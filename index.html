What choices are you making when you buy mobile Internet in India?

In India, less than 20 percent of the population has access to the Internet. At the same time, it is the second biggest mobile phone market. According to the Telecom Regulatory Autority of India (TRAI), nearly 90 percent of those who are on the Internet, access it through mobile. That also means that mobile telecom providers hold the future for user access. If a mobile provider decides to offer Facebook for a cheaper price, it can be anti-competitive in the larger scale.

What is at stake?

The idea of Net Neutrality comes with three major concepts:

  • All sites must be equally accessible: Airtel or Reliance cannot ban a site on their air waves.
  • The same access speed for each site at the telco/ISP level: Vodafone or Uninor cannot make YouTube load faster and slow down Vimeo.
  • The same data cost for access to each site (per KB/MB): What this interactive is about.

The Internet is a sea of opportunity and giving the edge to players such as Facebook and WhatsApp isn't playing fair. However, since it is a matter of consumer choice, you are free to explore your choices and what they might mean for everyday use.

When Reliance offers Facebook, Twitter and WhatsApp for free, it is giving a preference to these platforms as opposed to a sea of other places on the Internet. A young startup that aims to be the Twitter of India shall not emerge because the user has to pay to get on that platform. It is anti-competitive.

The same happens when you buy a Facebook only or WhatsApp only pack. However in this case, do see your choices on the other side. There is a possibility that you might be paying for nothing and this interactive helps you explore that.
